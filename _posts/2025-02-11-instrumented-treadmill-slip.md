This study — published in **_Scientific Reports_ (2025)** — is the first to
systematically quantify how the **intensity of a slip perturbation** affects
**backward slip-falls** induced by a **split-belt instrumented treadmill**.

Slips are a leading cause of injurious falls, and treadmill-based perturbation
systems are increasingly used both to study fall biomechanics and to train
reactive balance. A key open question remained: what perturbation intensity is
"enough" — strong enough to reveal fall mechanics or challenge balance, yet not
so strong that recovery becomes impossible? To answer this, we delivered
controlled, unpredictable forward-slip perturbations at **six intensities** —
three belt speeds (**0.5×, 1×, 2× preferred walking speed**) crossed with two
durations (**300 ms, 500 ms**) — to **24 healthy young adults**, and used a
harness load cell to identify falls and measure the peak loading force.

### Key findings

- **Speed drives falls.** The fall rate rose sharply with slipping speed
  regardless of duration (overall fall rate **76%**); relative fall risk
  increased **1.12–1.78×** as speed increased.
- **Duration matters mainly when fast.** Longer duration (500 ms vs 300 ms)
  significantly increased falls **only at 2×PWS**, where the difference in slip
  distance is largest (≈60 cm vs 100 cm).
- **Impact scales with speed, not duration.** Peak harness loading force
  increased with slipping speed (**≈43–51% of body weight**) but was unaffected
  by slip duration.
- **Design criteria.** These six intensities offer a practical map for tuning
  split-belt slip perturbations for fall-biomechanics research and
  perturbation-based fall-prevention training.
