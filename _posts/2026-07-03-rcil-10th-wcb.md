Predictive musculoskeletal (MSK) simulation is a powerful way to study how
humans move, but the muscular system is highly **redundant**, which makes
control hard. Imitation learning can reproduce recorded motion, yet learning
control **directly in muscle-activation space is slow, unstable, and prone to
collapse**.

Biology suggests a way out. **Equilibrium-point (EP) theory** [<a href="#ref-feldman1986">1</a>, <a href="#ref-feldman1966">2</a>]
proposes that the nervous system does not command muscle forces or activations
directly; instead, it specifies a **target equilibrium posture** — a
*referent configuration* — and lets the muscles' intrinsic length–tension
properties and stretch reflexes pull the limb toward that equilibrium on their
own. Movement, in this view, is produced by **shifting the equilibrium point**
over time, and the body settles toward each new target automatically. This turns
a hard, high-dimensional activation problem into the much smaller problem of
choosing *where* the equilibrium should be.

RCIL builds directly on this idea: it treats the **reference pose as the
equilibrium point** the body should move toward. But a raw reference is only an
approximate equilibrium — it ignores dynamics, external forces, and modeling
mismatch, so the true equilibrium needed to reproduce the motion is never
exactly the reference. Rather than relearn control from scratch, the policy only
has to learn the **small residual correction** that turns this imperfect
equilibrium into the right one.

Concretely, this is what **RCIL** does. It builds **predictive musculoskeletal
digital twins** of human walking that are controlled entirely in this
low-dimensional **referent space**: the policy never touches muscle activations,
it only outputs the residual **Δθ** on top of a **referent configuration** (the
target posture **R**). An **equilibrium-point reflex law** then converts that
target into activations automatically — exactly the muscle-level "settling"
behavior that EP theory describes.

Mechanically, at each step the target referent configuration *R* is formed by
adding the learned residual to the reference pose (with a 0.1 s anticipatory
lead), converted to a muscle-length threshold λ<sub>R</sub>, and turned into
activation *A(t)* by the equilibrium-point reflex law:

<div class="eq-block">
\[ R = \theta_{\text{ref}}(t + 0.1\,s) + \Delta\theta \]
\[ \lambda_R = \text{muscle fiber length at } R \]
\[ A(t) = \big[\, g\,\big( L(t) - (\lambda_R - \delta) + \mu\,V(t) \big) \,\big]_0^1 \]
  <div class="eq-legend">
    <span><strong>L(t)</strong> muscle fiber length</span>
    <span><strong>g</strong> length-based (tonic) gain</span>
    <span><strong>δ</strong> baseline muscle tone</span>
    <span><strong>V(t)</strong> muscle fiber velocity</span>
    <span><strong>μ</strong> velocity-based (phasic) gain</span>
    <span><strong>(λ<sub>R</sub> − δ)</strong> length threshold</span>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="eq-note">The activation is recomputed every physics step from the current fiber length, so the reflex law provides built-in, self-stabilizing feedback.</p>

The residual policy (SAC) is trained with a **tracking reward** that keeps the
simulated motion close to the recorded reference. The adopted reward combines
exponential tracking terms for the pelvis, per-plane joint pose, and foot
velocity, minus a fall penalty:

<div class="eq-block">
\[
\begin{aligned}
& r = \underbrace{r_{\text{pel}} + r_{\text{plane}} + r_{\text{ee}}}_{\text{tracking}} \;-\; r_{\text{fall}} \\
& r_{\text{pel}} = w_{\text{vel}}\,e^{\,k\,E^{\text{pel}}_{\text{vel}}} + w_{\text{ml}}\,e^{\,k\,E^{\text{pel}}_{\text{ml}}} + w_{\text{ori}}\,e^{\,k\,E^{\text{pel}}_{\text{ori}}} \\
& r_{\text{plane}} = \sum_{p\,\in\,\{\text{sag},\,\text{fro},\,\text{rot}\}} w_{p}\,\big( w_{\text{pos}}\,e^{\,k\,E^{p}_{\text{pos}}} + w_{\text{vel}}\,e^{\,k\,E^{p}_{\text{vel}}} \big) \\
& r_{\text{ee}} = e^{\,k\,E^{\text{ee}}_{\text{vel}}}
\end{aligned}
\]
</div>

where each term means:

- **r<sub>pel</sub> — pelvis:** translational velocity, mediolateral position, and orientation (tilt / list / rotation).
- **r<sub>plane</sub> — joint pose:** sagittal / frontal / rotation planes; each plane is a weighted sum of position and velocity exponentials (pos:vel = **2.5 : 1**), with per-joint errors variance-normalized within the plane.
- **r<sub>ee</sub> — feet:** pelvis-relative foot **velocity** only (foot-position tracking is OFF).
- **Shape:** every exponential has the form **exp(k·E)**, where *E* is a mean squared tracking error — it reaches its maximum (= 1) when the error is zero and decays as the motion drifts from the reference.
- **Fall:** a fall incurs a penalty **r<sub>fall</sub>** and ends the episode (early termination).

<div class="eq-block eq-block--text">
  <p class="eq-block-title">Training setup</p>
  <ul class="detail-list">
    <li><strong>Algorithm:</strong> Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), Stable-Baselines3</li>
    <li><strong>Hardware:</strong> 1&times; NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3070 GPU, Intel Core i9-14900K CPU</li>
    <li><strong>Parallel environments:</strong> 8 musculoskeletal simulations (identical reward &amp; simulation settings for all runs)</li>
  </ul>
</div>

Taken together, this referent-control formulation lowers the search dimension
and stabilizes imitation learning, so the twin converges to stable walking far
faster than learning in muscle-activation space. And because the policy only
nudges *where* the equilibrium should be — rather than dictating raw activations
— the muscles keep their built-in "settle toward the equilibrium" behavior. This
opens the door to **human-like, emergent responses to external forces** (e.g.,
wearable-robot assistance), a possibility we aim to verify against
**experimental data** in future work.
